Password Security

Essential Password Guidelines

  • Use a unique password for each account to prevent credential stuffing attacks
  • Create strong passwords with at least 12 characters, including uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols
  • Consider using passphrases that are easier to remember but still secure
  • Use a password manager to securely store and generate strong passwords
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) whenever possible
  • Change passwords immediately if you suspect a breach

Phishing Protection

Phishing attacks are among the most common cyber threats, attempting to trick you into revealing sensitive information or installing malware.

How to Identify Phishing Attempts

  • Be suspicious of unexpected emails, messages, or calls asking for personal information
  • Check for misspellings, grammatical errors, or unusual phrasing
  • Hover over links to verify their actual destination before clicking
  • Be wary of urgent requests creating pressure to act quickly
  • Verify sender email addresses carefully for slight misspellings
  • Be suspicious if the greeting is generic (e.g., "Dear Customer") rather than using your name

If You Suspect a Phishing Attempt

DO

  • Delete suspicious emails
  • Report phishing attempts to your IT department or service provider
  • Contact companies directly using their official website or phone number

DON'T

  • Click on suspicious links or open attachments
  • Provide personal, financial, or login information
  • Call phone numbers provided in suspicious messages

Common Phishing Scenarios

Type Description Warning Signs
Email Phishing Fraudulent emails claiming to be from legitimate organizations Mismatched URLs, generic greetings, urgency, spelling errors
Spear Phishing Targeted attacks using personal information Unusually personal, yet something feels "off" about the request
Smishing SMS text message phishing Unexpected texts with links, urgent requests for information
Vishing Voice phishing via phone calls Robotic voices, caller asking for sensitive information

Device Security

Computer Security

  • Keep your operating system and software updated
  • Use antivirus/anti-malware software and keep it updated
  • Enable firewall protection
  • Use disk encryption to protect sensitive data
  • Create regular backups of important data
  • Lock your computer when away from it
  • Use standard user accounts for daily tasks, not administrator accounts

Mobile Device Security

  • Set a strong PIN, password, or use biometric authentication
  • Enable remote tracking and wiping capabilities
  • Only download apps from official app stores
  • Review app permissions before installing
  • Keep your device updated with security patches
  • Use a VPN when connecting to public Wi-Fi
  • Disable Bluetooth and Wi-Fi when not in use

Safe Browsing

Browsing Safety Tips

  • Verify website security by looking for HTTPS and the padlock icon
  • Use private browsing when on public computers
  • Clear browsing history and cookies regularly
  • Be cautious when clicking on advertisements
  • Keep your browser and extensions updated
  • Use a VPN when accessing sensitive information on public networks
  • Be wary of sites asking for excessive personal information

Recommended Browser Extensions for Security

Social Media Safety

Social media platforms can expose your personal information to various risks if not used carefully.

Best Practices for Social Media

  • Review and limit the personal information you share on social media
  • Use strong, unique passwords for each social media account
  • Enable two-factor authentication for all platforms that offer it
  • Regularly review and update privacy settings
  • Be selective about friend/connection requests
  • Be careful about clicking links in messages, even from friends
  • Avoid posting about your current location or travel plans
  • Be mindful of the photos you share and what information they might reveal
  • Regularly review apps and third-party services connected to your accounts

Ransomware Prevention

What is Ransomware?

Ransomware is malicious software that encrypts your files and demands payment, usually in cryptocurrency, to restore access to your data.

Prevention Strategies

Data Protection

  • Maintain regular backups (3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 different media types, 1 offsite)
  • Keep backups disconnected from your network
  • Test backup restoration procedures regularly

Infection Prevention

  • Keep systems and software updated
  • Use reputable antivirus and anti-malware software
  • Be cautious with email attachments and links
  • Disable macros in Microsoft Office documents

If You're Infected

  1. Disconnect from the network immediately to prevent spread
  2. Report the incident to law enforcement
  3. Do not pay the ransom (payment doesn't guarantee recovery and funds criminal activities)
  4. Restore from clean backups after ensuring the infection is removed
  5. Consult with cybersecurity professionals for assistance

Identity Theft Protection

Safeguarding Your Identity

Preventive Measures

  • Regularly monitor your credit reports from all major bureaus
  • Consider placing a credit freeze or fraud alert on your credit files
  • Shred documents containing personal information before disposal
  • Use secure, unique passwords for financial accounts
  • Be cautious about sharing personal information online
  • Regularly check bank and credit card statements for unauthorized transactions

Warning Signs of Identity Theft

  • Unexplained withdrawals or charges on your accounts
  • Failing to receive expected bills or mail
  • Receiving calls from debt collectors about debts you don't owe
  • Finding unfamiliar accounts or charges on your credit report
  • Receiving notification about a data breach involving your information
  • Being denied credit for no apparent reason

If You've Been a Victim:

  1. Place a fraud alert with the credit bureaus
  2. Contact your financial institutions
  3. Change passwords for all accounts
  4. Report the theft to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC)
  5. File a police report
  6. Consider an extended credit freeze

Children's Online Safety

Protecting children online requires a combination of technical controls, education, and open communication.

Guidelines for Parents and Guardians

  • Use parental controls and content filtering on devices and home networks
  • Keep computers and devices in common areas of the home
  • Teach children about online privacy and the permanence of what they post
  • Discuss the dangers of sharing personal information online
  • Establish clear rules about acceptable online behavior and screen time
  • Teach children to recognize online scams and suspicious behavior
  • Monitor your child's online activities and friends
  • Keep communication open so children feel comfortable discussing concerns
  • Be aware of warning signs of cyberbullying or online exploitation
  • Regularly review privacy settings on social media accounts and games

Recommended Resources for Parents:

  • Common Sense Media (for reviews of apps, games, websites)
  • National Center for Missing & Exploited Children's NetSmartz (education)
  • Internet Matters (age-specific guidance)
  • Family Online Safety Institute (tools and research)
